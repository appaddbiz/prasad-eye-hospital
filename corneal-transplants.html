Corneal Transplants / Keratoplasty

Introduction

Corneal transplantation, also known as keratoplasty, is a surgical procedure that replaces a damaged or diseased cornea with a healthy donor cornea. This procedure is often recommended for patients with severe corneal diseases, scarring, or conditions like keratoconus that significantly impair vision.

Understanding the Cornea and Its Importance

The cornea is the clear, dome-shaped front part of the eye that plays a crucial role in focusing light onto the retina. Any damage, infection, or degenerative condition affecting the cornea can lead to blurry vision, glare sensitivity, and even blindness. When non-surgical treatments such as medications and specialized contact lenses fail, a corneal transplant becomes a viable solution.

Types of Corneal Transplants

There are different types of corneal transplant procedures depending on the extent of the damage and the specific condition of the patient. Dr. Shiva Prasad Kakarla, an experienced Eye Specialist in Krishna Nagar Layout, determines the most appropriate transplant technique based on individual patient needs.

Penetrating Keratoplasty (PK)

  • A full-thickness corneal transplant where the damaged cornea is completely replaced with a donor cornea.
  • Recommended for severe corneal scarring or advanced keratoconus.
  • Longer recovery time compared to partial transplants.

Deep Anterior Lamellar Keratoplasty (DALK)

  • A partial-thickness transplant that replaces only the outer and middle layers of the cornea, preserving the inner endothelial layer.
  • Reduces the risk of graft rejection compared to PK.
  • Suitable for patients with keratoconus or corneal scarring without endothelial damage.

Endothelial Keratoplasty (EK)

  • Includes procedures like Descemet's Stripping Automated Endothelial Keratoplasty (DSAEK) and Descemet's Membrane Endothelial Keratoplasty (DMEK).
  • Replaces only the innermost layer of the cornea, ideal for conditions like Fuchs' dystrophy and endothelial dysfunction.
  • Faster recovery and better visual outcomes than full-thickness transplants.

The Corneal Transplant Procedure

The corneal transplant procedure involves several critical steps:

Pre-Surgical Evaluation

  • A comprehensive eye examination, including corneal measurements and imaging, is performed.
  • Donor tissue matching and health screening to ensure compatibility.

Surgical Procedure

  • The surgery is typically performed under local or general anesthesia.
  • The damaged corneal tissue is carefully removed, and the donor cornea is precisely placed and sutured.
  • For endothelial keratoplasty, an air bubble technique is used to position the new corneal layer without stitches.

Post-Surgical Care and Recovery

  • Eye drops and medications are prescribed to prevent infection and inflammation.
  • Regular follow-up visits to monitor healing and visual improvement.
  • Avoiding strenuous activities and protecting the eye from injury during the recovery period.
